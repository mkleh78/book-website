Understanding volatility

Volatility expresses the expected range of price movement. In options, implied volatility (IV) is forward-looking and directly drives option premiums. High IV inflates premiums for sellers; low IV makes long options cheaper.

Key metrics: IV, HV, IV Rank

  • Implied Volatility (IV): Market’s forecast of future movement embedded in option prices.
  • Historical Volatility (HV): Realized past price movement.
  • IV Rank / IV Percentile: Compares current IV to the past year. IV Rank above 50 favors premium selling; below 20 favors premium buying.

Core strategies by volatility level

High IV (> 40 IV Rank): favor premium selling

  • Short strangles/straddles: Best on highly liquid underlyings; keep deltas small and manage aggressively.
  • Iron condors: Defined risk alternative with capped losses.
  • Wide bull/bear spreads: Collect credit with generous strike distance.

Medium IV (20–40 IV Rank): balanced approaches

  • Covered calls and cash secured puts: Earn premium while positioning for stock ownership.
  • Diagonal spreads: Sell short-term premium against a longer-dated option to capture theta while keeping directional exposure.
  • Calendars: Profit from differing time decay between expirations.

Low IV (< 20 IV Rank): favor premium buying

  • Long calls/puts: Cheaper directional bets when IV is subdued.
  • Debit spreads: Define risk and reduce cost versus outright longs.
  • Backspreads: Benefit from sudden volatility expansion.

Trading earnings and events

Earnings announcements often cause IV to spike beforehand and collapse afterward. Common approaches:

  • Short straddles/strangles: Collect rich premium but require strict risk controls and small size.
  • Iron condors: Define maximum loss while still capturing elevated premiums.
  • Post-earnings long options: Consider buying options after the IV crush if you expect a follow-through move.

Risk control with the Greeks

  • Delta: Keep net delta aligned with your directional view; neutralize if you just want to harvest volatility.
  • Gamma: High around expiration and at-the-money; reduce size or roll early to avoid large swings.
  • Theta: Time decay benefits sellers; buyers should choose enough duration to fight rapid decay.
  • Vega: Short vega profits from falling IV; long vega benefits from rising IV – match this to the regime.

Practical examples

High IV example: Sell an iron condor 45 DTE on a liquid index when IV Rank is 60, positioning short strikes near the 15–20 delta. Close at 50% max profit or roll if tested.
Low IV example: Buy a 60 DTE call debit spread on a quality stock when IV Rank is 15, choosing strikes that target a moderate move and keep risk defined.

Volatility playbook

  • Scan IV Rank daily and match strategy to the current regime.
  • Prioritize liquid underlyings with tight spreads.
  • Size positions so a single loss cannot damage the account.
  • Manage at 25–50% profits for short-vol trades to avoid late-stage gamma risk.
  • Give long-vol trades enough time; avoid near-dated options that decay too quickly.

Conclusion

Volatility is not the enemy – it is the fuel of options pricing. By measuring IV accurately and pairing it with the right strategy, you can systematically turn price swings into opportunity while controlling risk. Build a repeatable playbook, stay disciplined, and let volatility work for you.

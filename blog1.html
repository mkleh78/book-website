Options: Understanding the basics

Options are financial derivatives that give investors the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset (usually stocks) at a predetermined price within a specific time frame. This flexibility makes them powerful instruments for different market scenarios.

There are two basic types of options:

  • Call options: Give the buyer the right to purchase the underlying asset at the agreed strike price. Call options benefit from rising prices of the underlying.
  • Put options: Give the buyer the right to sell the underlying asset at the agreed strike price. Put options benefit from falling prices of the underlying.

Each option involves two parties:

  • The buyer (long position): acquires the right and pays a premium for it.
  • The seller (short position): assumes the obligation and receives the premium.
Good to know: Options have an expiration date. After that date they expire worthless. The time value of an option decreases as the expiration date approaches – a phenomenon known as time decay or theta decay.

Advantages of options trading for private investors

Why should individual investors even consider options trading? Here are some compelling reasons:

  • Leverage: With relatively little capital you can profit from the price movements of a more expensive underlying.
  • Income generation: Selling options allows you to earn regular premium income – similar to rental income on real estate.
  • Hedging: Options can act as “insurance” for your existing portfolio to limit losses in volatile markets.
  • Flexibility: Options let you profit from rising, falling, or sideways markets.
  • Defined risk: With certain strategies your maximum loss is known and limited in advance.

The 4 best beginner strategies in detail

Four strategies are especially suitable for beginners. They offer a balanced mix of complexity, risk, and return potential.

1. Cash Secured Put (CSP)

The cash secured put is one of the most popular beginner strategies and is perfect for investors who would like to buy a stock anyway but at a more attractive price.

How it works: You sell a put option on a stock and set aside enough cash to buy the stock if the price falls below the strike price. You receive a premium for selling the option.

Example: Company XYZ’s stock is trading at €100. You would like to buy it at €90. You sell a put option with a strike price of €90 and receive a premium of €3 per share. Two scenarios are possible:

1. The stock stays above €90: The option expires worthless and you keep the €3 premium.
2. The stock falls below €90: You must buy the stock at €90, but you already received €3, reducing your effective entry price to €87.
Advantages:
  • Regular income from premiums
  • Opportunity to buy shares at a lower price
  • Higher probability of success than directly buying options
  • Relatively easy to understand and execute
Disadvantages:
  • Ties up a lot of capital (cash as collateral)
  • Significant losses if prices drop sharply
  • Limited profit potential (maximum is the received premium)

2. Covered Call (CC)

The covered call is ideal for investors who already own shares and want to generate additional income. This strategy can significantly increase a portfolio’s total return.

How it works: You sell call options on shares you already own. You receive a premium for doing so. If the stock price is below the strike price at expiration, the option expires worthless and you keep both the shares and the premium. If the price is above the strike, you must sell the shares at the strike price.

Example: You own 100 shares of company ABC at €50 each. You sell a call option with a strike price of €55 and receive a premium of €2 per share. Two scenarios are possible:

1. The stock stays below €55: The option expires worthless; you keep your shares and the €200 premium.
2. The stock rises above €55, say to €60: You must sell your shares at €55 but realize a profit of €5 per share plus the €2 premium.
Advantages:
  • Generates regular additional income from existing positions
  • Lowers the cost basis of the held shares
  • Provides some protection in mildly falling markets
  • Works well in sideways markets
Disadvantages:
  • Caps the stock’s upside potential
  • Does not protect against major price drops
  • May lead to an unwanted sale if the stock rallies
  • Higher tax and transaction complexity

3. Bull Put Spread

The bull put spread is a strategy for investors expecting moderately rising or sideways markets but wanting to deploy less capital than with a cash secured put. This spread strategy limits both risk and potential gain.

How it works: You sell a put with a higher strike price and simultaneously buy a put with a lower strike price on the same underlying and expiration date. Because the sold option is worth more than the purchased one, you receive a net premium.

Example: Company DEF’s stock trades at €100. You sell a put with a €95 strike for €3 and simultaneously buy a put with an €85 strike for €1. Your net premium is €2 per share. Possible scenarios:

1. The stock stays above €95: Both options expire worthless; you keep the €2 net premium.
2. The stock falls to €90: The sold put is exercised, the purchased put expires worthless. Your loss is €3 (€95 - €90 - €2 premium).
3. The stock falls below €85: Both puts gain value, but the loss is capped at €8 (€95 - €85 - €2 premium).
Advantages:
  • Lower capital requirement than cash secured puts
  • Limited risk thanks to the purchased put
  • High probability of success in sideways markets
  • Flexible to adapt to different risk appetites
Disadvantages:
  • Lower premium income than simply selling puts
  • More complex strategy that requires greater understanding
  • Higher transaction costs because two options are involved
  • The spread can be difficult to execute if liquidity is low

4. Bull Call Spread

The bull call spread is ideal for investors who are bullish but want to limit risk. This strategy lets you benefit from rising markets without taking on the full risk of buying a call outright.

How it works: You buy a call with a lower strike price and simultaneously sell a call with a higher strike price on the same underlying and expiration date. Because the purchased call is more expensive than the sold call, you pay a net premium.

Example: A stock trades at €100. You buy a call with a €100 strike for €5 and sell a call with a €110 strike for €2. Your net cost is €3 per share. Possible scenarios:

1. The stock stays below €100: Both options expire worthless; you lose your €3 investment.
2. The stock rises to €105: Your purchased call is worth €5, the sold call expires worthless. You make a €2 profit (€5 - €3 cost).
3. The stock rises above €110: Your maximum profit is capped at €7 (€110 - €100 - €3 cost).
Advantages:
  • Lower cost than directly buying a call
  • Limited risk (maximum is the net premium paid)
  • Higher profit potential than pure premium strategies
  • Benefits from rising prices
Disadvantages:
  • Capped profit potential
  • Requires a correct directional view
  • Not suitable for strong upward moves (due to capped gains)
  • Time decay works against the position

Basics of option pricing

The price of an option – also called the premium – is influenced by several factors that are summarized in the so-called Greeks:

  • Delta: Measures how much the option price changes when the underlying price changes by €1.
  • Theta: Measures the time decay per day – how much value the option loses simply as time passes.
  • Vega: Shows how sensitive the option is to changes in implied volatility.
  • Gamma: Indicates how much the delta changes when the underlying price moves.
  • Rho: Measures the option’s sensitivity to interest rate changes.

The most important factor for beginners is implied volatility (IV). Higher IV leads to higher option prices because larger price swings are expected. Selling options is usually more attractive when IV is high; buying options when IV is low.

Which strategy fits which market conditions?

Different market phases require different strategies:

  • Bull markets: Bull call spread, covered call
  • Sideways markets: Covered call, cash secured put, bull put spread
  • Volatile markets: Bull put spread (with larger distances), longer-dated strategies
  • Bearing markets: Alternative strategies such as protective puts or bear call spreads may be more suitable (beyond the scope of this beginner guide)

Effective risk management in options trading

Good risk management is the key to long-term success. Pay special attention to the following points:

  • Position sizing: Risk no more than 1–3% of your trading capital per trade.
  • Diversification: Spread risk across different strategies, underlyings, and expirations.
  • Exit strategy: Set clear rules for closing a position (profit target, stop-loss, time-based exit).
  • Liquidity: Trade only liquid options with tight spreads and sufficient volume.
  • Volatility: Consider implied volatility – preferably sell when it is high and buy when it is low.

Common beginner mistakes and how to avoid them

Even experienced investors can make mistakes with options. Avoid these common pitfalls:

  • Over-leveraging: Do not risk too much capital in a single trade.
  • Ignoring risk management: Every trade needs a clear exit plan.
  • Lack of diversification: Spread your risk across different strategies and underlyings.
  • Neglecting liquidity: Trade only options with sufficient liquidity (tight spreads, high volume).
  • Ignoring volatility: Pay attention to implied volatility – avoid buying when it is high and selling when it is low.
  • Strategies that are too complex: Master the basics before moving on to more complex approaches.
  • Forgetting time decay: Time decay accelerates in the last 30–45 days before expiration.
  • Emotional decisions: Stick to your plan and do not be guided by greed or fear.

Next steps for aspiring options traders

How should you proceed if you want to start trading options?

  1. Deepen your education: Read books, attend webinars, and use educational resources from brokers.
  2. Use an options simulator: Practice with a paper trading account before risking real money.
  3. Start small: Begin with a single strategy and small position sizes.
  4. Keep a trading journal: Document your trades to learn from successes and mistakes.
  5. Find a mentor: Join options trading groups or find an experienced trader as a mentor.
  6. Stay informed: Continuously follow market trends and new strategies.
Important: The strategies presented in this article are for educational purposes. Every investor should conduct their own due diligence and seek professional advice if needed.

Conclusion

Options trading does not have to be complicated. With the four beginner strategies presented – cash secured put, covered call, bull put spread, and bull call spread – you have solid tools to diversify your portfolio and potentially improve your returns.

The key to success lies in thorough education, consistent risk management, and selecting the right strategy for the prevailing market environment. Start small, keep learning, and build your skills step by step.
